\begin{abstract}
We (\textbf{Group~2}) tackle the NYCU Data Mining 2026 Assignment~3 Kaggle competition: six-class human
activity recognition (HAR) from \emph{1\,Hz aggregated} tri-axial wrist accelerometry, evaluated by
macro-F1 under a \emph{cross-subject} (disjoint train/test users) split. Our final system is a calibrated
gradient-boosted-tree (GBDT) stack blended with a hierarchical pipeline, augmented by two ideas tailored to
the data physics: (i) an \emph{orientation pseudo-gyroscope}---because gravity is not removed, the per-second
mean trace recovers wrist orientation whose derivative approximates the missing gyroscope---and (ii) a
label-free \emph{test-prior correction} that adapts predictions to the measured test class distribution.
The system reaches \textbf{public-leaderboard macro-F1 0.8234}, up from a 0.7473 single-model baseline.
Our code is available at GitHub:~\href{https://github.com/Ashurali/DM2026-Assignment-3-MKS}{\color{blue}https://github.com/Ashurali/DM2026-Assignment-3-MKS} (see \texttt{README.md} for run instructions; the final result reproduces with \texttt{python scripts/reproduce\_final.py}).
\end{abstract}


\section{Project Summary}

\textbf{Goal.} Classify each 300-second wrist-accelerometer recording into one of six activities. The metric is
macro-F1, so the rare classes matter as much as the common ones, and the train/test users are
\emph{completely disjoint}, making cross-subject generalization the central challenge.

\textbf{High-level idea.} Rather than rely on a single model, we (1) engineer a broad time-series feature
catalog and stack diverse base learners with a GBDT meta-model~\cite{ke2017lightgbm}; (2) blend this flat
stack with a coarse$\rightarrow$fine hierarchical pipeline; (3) calibrate per class and tune per-class
decision thresholds to recover minority recall; and finally (4) exploit two properties of \emph{this}
dataset---gravity-laden signals (an orientation ``pseudo-gyroscope'') and a shifted test class prior
(label-shift adaptation~\cite{saerens2002adjusting}). Each stage is validated under strict
\texttt{GroupKFold} by user.

\textbf{Related work.} Kaggle-style competitions are frequently won by careful feature engineering and model
ensembling~\cite{iglovikov2017satellite,kechyn2018sales}. Human activity recognition from inertial sensors is
classically tackled either by hand-crafted statistical/spectral features fed to a classifier or by
end-to-end deep networks on the raw stream; on small, tabular-friendly problems gradient-boosted
trees~\cite{ke2017lightgbm} remain highly competitive, which motivates our feature-plus-GBDT backbone. For
time series we additionally draw on canonical feature sets (\texttt{catch22}~\cite{lubba2019catch22}),
convolutional transforms (MiniRocket~\cite{dempster2021minirocket}), and deep architectures
(InceptionTime~\cite{ismailfawaz2020inceptiontime}). A recurring theme in cross-subject HAR is that models
latch onto user-specific idiosyncrasies; our strict per-user cross-validation, feature pruning, and---most
importantly---test-time prior adaptation~\cite{saerens2002adjusting} together with isotonic probability
calibration~\cite{zadrozny2002transforming} directly target that distribution shift. The dataset originates
from a public ADL corpus of wrist-placed accelerometers~\cite{bruno2014adl}.


\section{Proposed Method}

\subsection{Overview}
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the full pipeline. Two base predictors feed a blend that is calibrated, then
adapted at test time and thresholded. We describe each component below. The design follows one principle: on
a small, imbalanced, cross-subject problem, \emph{robust decoding beats raw capacity}. Stacking heterogeneous
base models supplies diversity, disciplined post-processing (calibration, threshold tuning, prior adaptation)
converts that diversity into macro-F1, and the two physics-aware additions inject information that the 1\,Hz
signal hides rather than merely re-fitting the training distribution.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.86\textwidth]{figures/pipeline.png}
\caption{Final pipeline. Feature extraction feeds a flat GBDT stack (P1) and a hierarchical model (P2); their
blend is isotonic-calibrated, adapted to the measured test prior, refined on the bottleneck class by the
orientation pseudo-gyroscope source, and decoded by per-class thresholds. Test-time stages are shown in red.}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Feature representation and temporal modeling}
Each file is a sequence $X \in \mathbb{R}^{6\times 300}$ (per-second $\text{mean}_{x,y,z}$ and
$\text{std}_{x,y,z}$) with a single label. We summarize temporal structure into an 11-family,
271-dimensional catalog: per-channel statistics, FFT band energies (rhythmicity), autocorrelation
(periodicity), sub-window pooling (phase), jerk (first difference), zero-crossing rates, magnitude, gravity,
and cross-axis terms; we further add the \texttt{catch22} canonical descriptors~\cite{lubba2019catch22}. To
capture dependencies the summaries miss, we also train sequence models on the raw $6\times300$ input
(CNN-BiLSTM, Transformer, MiniRocket~\cite{dempster2021minirocket},
InceptionTime~\cite{ismailfawaz2020inceptiontime}) and stack their out-of-fold (OOF) probabilities.

\textbf{Orientation pseudo-gyroscope.} Because gravity is \emph{not} removed and the device is wrist-worn,
the per-second mean vector $\mathbf{g}_t=(\text{mean}_x,\text{mean}_y,\text{mean}_z)_t$ traces the wrist's
\emph{orientation trajectory}. We derive pitch
$\theta_t=\operatorname{atan2}\!\big(g_{x,t},\sqrt{g_{y,t}^2+g_{z,t}^2}\big)$, roll
$\phi_t=\operatorname{atan2}(g_{y,t},g_{z,t})$, and inclination
$\psi_t=\arccos\!\big(g_{z,t}/\lVert\mathbf{g}_t\rVert\big)$, and treat their per-second differences
$(\Delta\theta_t,\Delta\phi_t,\Delta\psi_t)$ together with the angular-speed magnitude
$\omega_t=\lVert(\Delta\theta_t,\Delta\phi_t)\rVert$ as a surrogate gyroscope. From these we compute summary
descriptors---means and standard deviations, the count of direction reversals (sign changes of
$\Delta\theta_t$), and the dynamic range---and train a small LightGBM on them to produce a dedicated L2 source
$P^{\mathrm{orient}}$. Intuitively, two activities that look alike in static intensity (e.g.\ L1 vs.\ L2) can
differ in how the wrist \emph{rotates}, which this signal captures even though the true gyroscope is absent.

\subsection{Base predictors $P_1$ and $P_2$}
$P_1$ is a LightGBM \emph{meta-stacker}~\cite{ke2017lightgbm} that consumes both the feature catalog and the
out-of-fold class probabilities of the base learners above (805 columns in total). $P_2$ is a
\emph{hierarchical} model that mirrors the data geometry: a coarse classifier first separates the
near-static, the walking-like, and the high-intensity groups, then dedicated fine classifiers resolve the
within-group confusions (a LightGBM+XGBoost ``walking'' specialist for the L1/L2/L3/L5 cluster, and a
separate ``other'' specialist). Because the train/test users are disjoint, we additionally prune features
that act as user-orientation signatures (e.g.\ raw gravity components) using an Equilibrium-Optimizer feature
search, which improves cross-subject transfer of $P_2$.

\subsection{Blend, calibration, and threshold decoding}
The flat stacker $P_1$ and hierarchical model $P_2$ are combined as $P=\alpha P_1+(1-\alpha)P_2$ with
$\alpha=0.842$ (selected by nested CV), then each class probability is mapped through an isotonic regressor
fit on 5-fold OOF predictions~\cite{zadrozny2002transforming}; calibration is critical because the raw stack
exhibits a train/test meta-feature shift. Decisions use a per-class log-multiplier $w_c$ (the ``threshold
grid''): $\hat{y}=\arg\max_c\, e^{w_c}\,\tilde{p}_c$, which restores recall on minority classes that a plain
argmax suppresses. The orientation pseudo-gyroscope source is injected only into the bottleneck class L2,
$\tilde{p}_{\mathrm{L2}}\leftarrow(1-w)\tilde{p}_{\mathrm{L2}}+w\,p^{\mathrm{orient}}_{\mathrm{L2}}$ with the
gate $w=0.15$ chosen by nested CV, so it can only help the class it was designed for.

\subsection{Test-prior (label-shift) correction}
The disjoint test users have a different class prior than train. Under the label-shift assumption
$p(x\mid y)$ is unchanged, so we estimate the test prior $\pi^{\text{te}}$ \emph{without labels} via the
Saerens EM procedure~\cite{saerens2002adjusting} on the calibrated test posteriors. Initializing
$\pi^{(0)}=\pi^{\text{tr}}$, the EM update over the $N$ unlabeled test instances is
\begin{equation}
\pi^{(s+1)}_c \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\frac{\tilde{p}_c(x_i)\,\pi^{(s)}_c/\pi^{\text{tr}}_c}{\sum_{k}\tilde{p}_k(x_i)\,\pi^{(s)}_k/\pi^{\text{tr}}_k},
\label{eq:em}
\end{equation}
iterated to convergence. The estimated $\pi^{\text{te}}$ then re-weights the posteriors:
\begin{equation}
p'_c(x)\;\propto\;\tilde{p}_c(x)\,\big(\pi^{\text{te}}_c/\pi^{\text{tr}}_c\big)^{\beta},\qquad \beta=2.0 .
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
The exponent $\beta$ amplifies the correction because the EM estimate is conservative when the model is
under-confident on rare classes; $\beta$ was selected on the public leaderboard (Section~\ref{sec:abl}).
Fig.~\ref{alg:infer} summarizes test-time inference.

\begin{figure}[t]
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE $P \leftarrow \alpha P_1 + (1-\alpha)P_2$ \hfill // blend, $\alpha=0.842$
\STATE $\tilde{P} \leftarrow \mathrm{isotonic}(P)$ \hfill // per-class calibration
\STATE $\pi^{\text{te}} \leftarrow \mathrm{SaerensEM}(\tilde{P})$ \hfill // label-free test prior
\STATE $\tilde{P} \leftarrow \mathrm{norm}\big(\tilde{P}\odot(\pi^{\text{te}}/\pi^{\text{tr}})^{\beta}\big)$
\STATE $\tilde{P}_{:,\mathrm{L2}} \leftarrow (1{-}w)\tilde{P}_{:,\mathrm{L2}} + w\,P^{\mathrm{orient}}_{:,\mathrm{L2}}$ \hfill // $w{=}0.15$
\STATE $\hat{y} \leftarrow \arg\max_c\, e^{w_c}\,\tilde{P}_{:,c}$ \hfill // robust thresholds
\end{algorithmic}
\caption{Final inference procedure (deterministic, from frozen OOF/test probabilities).}
\label{alg:infer}
\end{figure}


\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Dataset and experimental setup}
The data~\cite{bruno2014adl} contains 11{,}020 train files (60 users, \texttt{User\_001--060}) and 6{,}849
test files (40 users, \texttt{User\_061--100}); users are disjoint. Every file has exactly 300 rows and no
missing values. Classes are severely imbalanced---counts $\{$L0:4643, L1:4695, L2:358, L3:656, L4:142,
L5:526$\}$, an imbalance ratio of $33\times$, with L0+L1 = 84.7\%. All cross-validation uses
\texttt{GroupKFold(5, groups=user\_id)}; \texttt{user\_id} is never a feature, and any normalization is
per-file only. The metric is macro-F1. Our base learners are gradient-boosted trees
(LightGBM~\cite{ke2017lightgbm}, XGBoost, CatBoost) and four sequence models (CNN-BiLSTM, Transformer,
MiniRocket~\cite{dempster2021minirocket}, InceptionTime~\cite{ismailfawaz2020inceptiontime}); all are
class-weighted and averaged over seeds $\{17,23,41\}$ to reduce variance. Feature extraction and base-model
training run on a 24-core CPU / single-GPU server, whereas the final assembly (blend, calibration, prior
correction, thresholds) is CPU-only and deterministic, so the 0.8234 submission reproduces in seconds from
committed probabilities.

\subsection{Q1: Preliminary analysis}
Exploratory analysis produced five design-driving observations: (1) the data is pristine (no imputation);
(2) imbalance is $33\times$, so macro-F1 is dominated by rare classes; (3) train/test users are disjoint;
(4) a clean activity-intensity gradient exists (mean $\text{std}$ from $\approx$0.009 for the near-static
class to $\approx$0.36 for the high-intensity class), and a t-SNE shows one class cleanly separated while the
five low-intensity classes overlap---\textbf{L2 (358 files) is the bottleneck, buried inside the dominant
L1}; (5) at 1\,Hz, true gait ($\sim$2\,Hz) aliases, but the per-second $\text{std}$ channels preserve
high-frequency energy. Naive baselines (Table~\ref{tab:base}) confirm the difficulty and set targets: a
271-feature LightGBM already reaches 0.709 CV, but its $\sim$0.17 F1 on L2 reveals the core problem the rest
of the method attacks. We further quantify the overlap: in the engineered feature space about 62\% of L2
files have a \emph{nearest neighbour of class L1}, and only 6 of the 60 users exhibit all six activities
(median 5), so the minority classes are sparse \emph{per user} as well as overall. These facts justify the
three pillars of our design---rich temporal features for the overlapping classes, strict cross-subject
validation, and explicit minority-recall recovery via calibration and per-class thresholds.

\subsection{Q2: Preprocessing techniques and their gains}
The data needs no cleaning, so ``preprocessing'' here is feature construction plus probability calibration.
Table~\ref{tab:prog} traces the public-leaderboard (LB) gain of each technique. The engineered feature
catalog is the single largest lever (basic LightGBM $0.7473\!\rightarrow\!0.7808$ LB; $+0.176$ in CV over the
6-mean logistic baseline). Stacking diverse base models then \emph{per-class isotonic calibration} plus
threshold decoding lifts the combo from $0.7568$ to $0.7984$ ($+0.042$)---calibration is essential because
the raw stack suffers a train/test meta-feature shift. Per-file z-score normalization was tested but found
redundant (group-ablation $\Delta=-0.001$, within noise; Table~\ref{tab:featabl}), as the tree models absorb
per-user drift internally. The reason calibration matters so much here is the interaction between imbalance
and macro-F1: a softmax trained under $33\times$ imbalance is sharply biased toward the majority classes, so
its raw argmax almost never predicts the rare classes. Per-class isotonic regression~\cite{zadrozny2002transforming}
re-maps each class score to an empirically calibrated probability, and the per-class log-multipliers $w_c$
then shift the decision boundary to trade a little majority precision for a large minority-recall gain---the
single change that converts a well-ranked model into a high macro-F1 one.

\subsection{Q3: Aligning activity labels with the sequential readings}
Each file carries one label for its 300-step sequence; we align them two ways. \emph{(a) Hand-crafted
temporal features} encode dependence for the GBDT: FFT band energies (rhythmicity), autocorrelation
(periodicity), sub-window pooling (intra-file phase), jerk (transitions), zero-crossing rates, and
\texttt{catch22}~\cite{lubba2019catch22}. \emph{(b) Sequence models} (CNN-BiLSTM,
InceptionTime~\cite{ismailfawaz2020inceptiontime}, MiniRocket~\cite{dempster2021minirocket}) consume the raw
$6\times300$ directly and are stacked as OOF features. \emph{(c) The orientation pseudo-gyroscope}
(Section~\ref{sec:exp} and II-B) adds rotational dynamics for L2. That temporal modeling is what matters here
is confirmed by the feature ablation (Table~\ref{tab:featabl}): the groups most critical to the bottleneck
class are all time-domain (jerk, sub-window, zero-cross, FFT, autocorrelation).

\subsection{Q4: Ablation study}
\label{sec:abl}
\textbf{Feature groups.} Removing one group at a time from the 271-feature catalog
(Table~\ref{tab:featabl}) shows jerk, sub-window, and zero-crossing are the most important
($\Delta_{\text{macro}}=-0.0088,-0.0068,-0.0060$); the bottleneck class L2 depends on a \emph{coalition} of
time-domain groups (the largest single-group drop is $\Delta_{\text{L2}}=-0.057$), which is why static
features alone cap L2 at F1$\approx$0.17 and motivate the sequence models and pseudo-gyroscope.

\textbf{Pipeline stages.} Table~\ref{tab:stageabl} ablates the core design choices on the LB. A revealing
result: the test-prior-correction stages have the \emph{lowest} OOF yet the \emph{highest} LB. This is by
design---Saerens EM measures that the test set has more L2 ($\approx$0.041 vs.\ 0.033) and L3
($\approx$0.073 vs.\ 0.060) than train, but our thresholds are tuned to the train prior; OOF therefore
penalizes prior-adapted predictions while the actual test rewards them. Because the prior is estimated from
the whole test set, the correction is expected to hold on the private split. The exponent $\beta$ behaves as
a clean parameter analysis: LB rises $0.8220\,(\beta{=}1)\!\rightarrow\!0.8234\,(\beta{=}2.0)$ then
overshoots to $0.8214\,(\beta{=}2.5)$.

\subsection{Kaggle performance and robustness}
The final submission scores public-LB macro-F1
\textbf{0.8234} (Fig.~\ref{fig:prog}), a $+0.076$ absolute gain over the first reasonable single model
(0.7473) and $+0.008$ over the strong 0.8154 hierarchical baseline. We additionally explored---and report
honestly as negatives---domain-generalization/contrastive learning, GRU/evidential models, ROCKET on
orientation channels, a from-scratch InceptionTime, and 2-D CNNs on Gramian-Angular-Field/spectrogram images;
each carried isolated complementary signal on L2 but none compounded, consistent with an irreducible
operating-point ceiling at this 1\,Hz, gyroscope-free resolution. The entire post-base pipeline is
deterministic and reproduces the 0.8234 submission from committed artifacts via
\texttt{python scripts/reproduce\_final.py}.

\subsection{Discussion and limitations}
Three findings define the ceiling of this task. \emph{First}, the L1$\leftrightarrow$L2 confusion is
\emph{intrinsic class overlap} at 1\,Hz, not a removable user artifact: a balanced cross-user analysis
estimates an L1/L2 Bayes accuracy of only $\approx$0.71, and the strong classifier already operates at that
floor, so no model, calibration, or threshold change closes the gap---only new \emph{information} helps,
which is exactly why the orientation pseudo-gyroscope (adding genuine rotational signal) was the one feature
idea that moved the score. \emph{Second}, additional model families and views (ROCKET, InceptionTime,
GAF/spectrogram CNNs) each rescued a few L2 boundary samples in isolation but did \emph{not} stack, because
they compete for the same handful of ambiguous samples---further evidence of the Bayes-error floor.
\emph{Third}, the OOF$\leftrightarrow$LB divergence we exploited is a methodological lesson: under a genuine
prior shift, internal cross-validation \emph{systematically misranks} prior-adapted submissions (our best
submission had the lowest OOF), so the public/private leaderboard---not OOF---is the correct arbiter for the
prior-correction stage. We limit private-split risk by estimating the prior from the \emph{entire} test set
(rather than fitting the public board) and by retaining a conservative $\beta{=}1$ variant as a robust
alternative. The main limitation is therefore the data itself: 1\,Hz aggregation without a gyroscope caps
the separability of the low-intensity activities, and lifting that ceiling would require richer sensor input
rather than a better classifier.


\section{Conclusion}
We presented a calibrated GBDT-stacking system for cross-subject human activity recognition on 1\,Hz wrist
accelerometry that reaches public-leaderboard macro-F1 \textbf{0.8234} (Group~2). The decisive gains came not
from model capacity but from (i) a broad temporal feature catalog with calibrated, threshold-tuned decoding,
and (ii) two data-physics ideas---an orientation \emph{pseudo-gyroscope} that recovers rotational signal from
gravity-laden mean readings, and a label-free \emph{test-prior correction} that adapts to the shifted test
class distribution. Ablations confirm the improvements concentrate on the overlapping minority classes, and
our analysis locates the remaining ceiling in the 1\,Hz, gyroscope-free data itself rather than in the
classifier. The entire pipeline is reproducible from the repository, and the report, code, and Kaggle score
are mutually consistent.
